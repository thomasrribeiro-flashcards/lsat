# LSAT — Flaw Questions

## 10.1 What a Flaw Question Asks

Q: What does a Flaw-in-the-Reasoning question ask?
A: It asks you to identify the logical error the argument commits. The stimulus is a flawed argument; the correct answer describes the error in abstract terms. The most common LSAT question type after Strengthen.

C: A Flaw answer describes the [logical error] the argument commits, in abstract language.

## 10.2 Flaw Stems

Q: What stems indicate a Flaw question?
A: "The argument's reasoning is flawed in that it …" "Which describes a flaw in the reasoning?" "The argument is most vulnerable to criticism on which grounds?"

## 10.3 The Twelve Most Common LSAT Flaws

Q: Which canonical LSAT flaws misuse conditional or causal logic?
A: Sufficient/necessary confusion. Causation from correlation.

Q: Which canonical LSAT flaws mishandle an opponent's position or a word's meaning?
A: Ad hominem. Straw man. Equivocation.

Q: Which canonical LSAT flaws rest on bad evidence or bad sources?
A: Unrepresentative sample. Appeal to authority. Appeal to ignorance.

Q: Which canonical LSAT flaws are illegitimate structural moves?
A: Circular reasoning. Part-to-whole. Whole-to-part. False dichotomy.

## 10.4 Sufficient/Necessary Confusion

Q: What is sufficient/necessary confusion?
A: The argument treats a sufficient condition as if it were necessary, or vice versa — typically committing mistaken reversal or mistaken negation. The most common LSAT flaw.

C: Mistaken reversal and mistaken negation are the two forms of the [sufficient/necessary] confusion flaw.

## 10.5 Causation from Correlation

Q: What is the "causation from correlation" flaw?
A: The argument concludes one thing causes another based only on the two being correlated. Fails to consider reverse causation, common cause, or coincidence.

C: Concluding that one thing causes another merely because the two occur together commits the [correlation-causation] flaw.

## 10.6 Ad Hominem

Q: What is the ad hominem flaw?
A: The argument attacks the person making a claim rather than the claim itself. The person's character, motives, or interests are treated as evidence the claim is false — but they're logically irrelevant to truth.

C: Attacking the person rather than the argument is the [ad hominem] flaw.

## 10.7 Straw Man

Q: What is the straw man flaw?
A: The argument distorts an opponent's position into a weaker version, then refutes the distortion. The actual position remains untouched. Often appears as "Critics say X is bad, but X is actually good for Y reason" when critics never claimed X was bad in that way.

## 10.8 Equivocation

Q: What is equivocation?
A: The argument uses the same word in two different senses to make its conclusion follow. Common LSAT examples involve words like "law" (legal vs scientific), "natural" (in nature vs unprocessed), or "free" (no cost vs at liberty).

C: Using a word with two different meanings to make the argument work is the [equivocation] flaw.

## 10.9 Circular Reasoning

Q: What is circular reasoning?
A: The argument assumes its conclusion as one of its premises — sometimes restating the conclusion in different words. The premises don't independently support the conclusion because they presuppose it.

C: Assuming what the argument tries to prove is the [circular] reasoning flaw.

## 10.10 Unrepresentative Sample

Q: What is the unrepresentative sample flaw?
A: The argument generalizes from a sample that doesn't reflect the broader population. The sample may be too small, self-selected, or drawn from a non-representative subgroup.

C: Generalizing from a sample that doesn't reflect the population is the [unrepresentative sample] flaw.

## 10.11 Appeal to Authority

Q: What is the appeal-to-authority flaw?
A: The argument supports its conclusion by citing someone's opinion as evidence, when that person isn't actually an authority on the relevant question. Also appears when an authority is cited for a question outside their expertise.

## 10.12 Appeal to Ignorance

Q: What is the appeal-to-ignorance flaw?
A: The argument concludes a claim is true because it hasn't been disproven, or false because it hasn't been proven. Lack of evidence is not evidence of the contrary.

C: Concluding X is true because nobody has disproven it is the [appeal to ignorance] flaw.

## 10.13 Part-to-Whole

Q: What is the part-to-whole flaw?
A: The argument concludes a property of a whole based on a property of its parts. "Each player is excellent, so the team must be excellent" — but team performance depends on coordination, not just individual skill.

C: Inferring that a group has some property because each of its members has it is the [part-to-whole] flaw.

## 10.14 Whole-to-Part

Q: What is the whole-to-part flaw?
A: The argument concludes a property of a part based on a property of its whole. "The university is wealthy, so this department must be wealthy" — but wealth at one level doesn't imply wealth at another.

## 10.15 False Dichotomy

Q: What is the false-dichotomy flaw?
A: The argument presents two options as the only possibilities and rules out one to conclude the other, when other options exist. Often signaled by "either … or …".

C: Treating two options as the only alternatives when others exist is the [false dichotomy] flaw.

## 10.16 Composition vs Equivocation

Q: How is composition different from equivocation?
A: Composition (part-to-whole) shifts from member to group; equivocation shifts from one meaning of a word to another. Both involve illegitimate transfer, but composition is about quantity/level while equivocation is about meaning.

## 10.17 Percentage vs Number Confusion

Q: What is the percentage-vs-number flaw?
A: The argument conflates a percentage and an absolute number. A growing percentage doesn't imply a growing count if the population shrinks; a higher count doesn't imply a higher percentage if the population grew faster.

C: Assuming a rising share implies a rising count (or vice versa) commits the [percentage-vs-number] flaw.

## 10.18 Survey Self-Selection

Q: What is the survey self-selection flaw?
A: The argument relies on a survey where respondents chose whether to participate. People who self-select into surveys often differ systematically from non-respondents, biasing results toward people with strong opinions.

## 10.19 Pre-Phrasing Flaws

P: How do you efficiently work a Flaw question?
S: Apply IPEE.

**IDENTIFY** — Read the stimulus actively, looking for a logical leap. Common signals: a sweeping conclusion from narrow evidence; a causal claim from a correlation; a "necessary" condition treated as sufficient.

**PLAN** — Name the flaw in your own words before reading answers. Match it to one of the canonical types.

**EXECUTE** — Find the answer that abstractly describes that flaw. Eliminate answers that describe flaws the argument doesn't commit, even if those flaws sound bad.

**EVALUATE** — Confirm the chosen answer maps onto something the argument actually does. Watch for "describes a flaw" vs "names a flaw the argument does not commit" — both look superficially similar in stems.

## 10.20 Trap: Right Flaw, Wrong Argument

Q: What is the "right flaw, wrong argument" trap?
A: The answer accurately describes a flaw type, but the argument doesn't commit that specific error. LSAT writers exploit familiarity — students see a recognized flaw name and pick it without verifying.

## 10.21 Trap: Describes Argument but Not a Flaw

Q: What is the "describes the argument" trap on flaw questions?
A: The answer accurately describes something the argument did but doesn't identify why doing it was a logical error. A correct flaw answer must describe both what was done and why it's wrong.
