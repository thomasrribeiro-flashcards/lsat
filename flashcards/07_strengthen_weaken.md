# LSAT — Strengthen, Weaken, Evaluate

## 7.1 Why These Are the Most Common LR Questions

Q: Why do strengthen and weaken questions appear so frequently?
A: They directly test the skill law schools care about: assessing whether new information helps or hurts an argument. Together they make up roughly a third of every Logical Reasoning section.

## 7.2 Strengthen Question Defined

Q: What does a Strengthen question ask?
A: It asks which answer choice, if true, would make the conclusion more likely to follow from the premises. The correct answer doesn't need to prove the conclusion — it just needs to make the argument stronger than it was.

C: A Strengthen answer makes the conclusion [more likely] to follow, even if it doesn't prove it.

## 7.3 Weaken Question Defined

Q: What does a Weaken question ask?
A: It asks which answer choice, if true, would make the conclusion less likely to follow. The correct answer doesn't need to disprove the conclusion — it just needs to undermine the support.

C: A Weaken answer makes the conclusion [less likely], even if it doesn't disprove it.

## 7.4 Strengthen Stems

Q: What stems indicate a Strengthen question?
A: "Which of the following, if true, most strengthens the argument?" "Which most helps to justify the reasoning?" "Which provides the most support for the conclusion?" The keywords are "support", "strengthen", "justify".

## 7.5 Weaken Stems

Q: What stems indicate a Weaken question?
A: "Which of the following, if true, most weakens the argument?" "Which most undermines the conclusion?" "Which casts most doubt on the reasoning?" Keywords: "weaken", "undermine", "cast doubt", "call into question".

## 7.6 The "If True" Convention

Q: Why do strengthen and weaken stems include "if true"?
A: It tells you to accept each answer choice as a fact, even if it sounds implausible. Don't reject answers because they seem unlikely — your job is to evaluate their effect on the argument assuming they are true.

C: The phrase "if true" tells you to [accept] each answer choice as fact when evaluating its effect.

## 7.7 Pre-Phrasing Strengthen Answers

P: How do you pre-phrase the correct answer to a Strengthen question?
S: Apply IPEE.

**IDENTIFY** — Locate the conclusion and the premises that supposedly support it. Note the gap or assumption.

**PLAN** — Ask: "What new fact would close the gap?" For causal arguments, eliminate alternative causes. For statistical arguments, validate the sample. For analogies, show the analog is genuinely similar.

**EXECUTE** — Scan answers for matches. The correct answer often supplies the assumption you identified — or rules out an objection.

**EVALUATE** — Verify the answer affects the conclusion specifically, not background facts. Reject answers that strengthen a related claim but not the actual conclusion.

## 7.8 Pre-Phrasing Weaken Answers

Q: How do you pre-phrase a weaken answer?
A: Identify the assumption and ask "what would make this assumption false?" For causal arguments, propose an alternative cause or reverse causation. For statistical arguments, attack the sample. For analogies, show a relevant difference.

## 7.9 Strengthen ≠ Prove

Q: Why is "strengthen" not the same as "prove"?
A: Many test-takers reject correct strengthen answers because they don't establish the conclusion beyond doubt. The standard is incremental — the answer must move the needle in the right direction, not move it all the way.

## 7.10 Weaken ≠ Disprove

Q: Why is "weaken" not the same as "disprove"?
A: A weakener only needs to reduce confidence in the conclusion. Demanding that the answer destroy the argument leads you to skip correct answers in favor of impossibly strong wrong ones.

## 7.11 Evaluate the Argument

Q: What does an "Evaluate the Argument" question ask?
A: It asks which question, if answered, would be most useful in assessing the argument. The correct answer is a question whose two possible answers would push the argument in opposite directions.

C: The correct Evaluate answer is a question whose two possible answers would [strengthen] and [weaken] the argument respectively.

## 7.12 The Variance Test

Q: How do you confirm an Evaluate answer?
A: Ask both possible answers to the question. If "yes" strengthens and "no" weakens (or vice versa), the question is useful. If both answers leave the argument unchanged, the question is irrelevant.

C: An Evaluate answer passes the [Variance Test]: opposite answers must produce opposite effects on the argument.

## 7.13 Trap: Reverse Direction

Q: What is the "reverse" trap on strengthen/weaken?
A: A correct-feeling answer that actually has the opposite effect — strengthens when you needed to weaken, or vice versa. Always confirm the direction by re-reading the question stem before locking in.

## 7.14 Trap: Out-of-Scope

Q: What is the out-of-scope trap?
A: An answer that introduces information unrelated to the specific premises and conclusion of the argument. It might be true and even relevant to the topic, but it doesn't move the specific reasoning at issue.

## 7.15 Trap: Half-Right

Q: What is the half-right trap?
A: An answer whose first clause is correct but whose second clause is wrong (or vice versa). LSAT writers exploit reading momentum — if the start sounds right, students stop scrutinizing the rest.

## 7.16 Strengthen Principles

Q: How do principle-based strengthen questions work?
A: A "principle that helps to justify the reasoning" answer is a general rule which, when applied to the specific situation, sanctions the conclusion. The principle must be broad enough to cover the case but specific enough to apply.

## 7.17 Weakening Conditional Arguments

Q: How do you weaken a conditional argument?
A: Show the conditional doesn't apply to the case at hand, or that the sufficient condition isn't actually present, or that the case is an exception. You can rarely "weaken" the conditional itself — instead, attack its application.

## 7.18 Strengthening Survey Arguments

Q: How do you strengthen an argument based on a survey?
A: Confirm the sample is representative, the response rate is high, the questions weren't biased, and the result generalizes to the conclusion's target population. Each addresses a different survey-validity attack.
