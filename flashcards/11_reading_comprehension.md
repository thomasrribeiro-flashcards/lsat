# LSAT — Reading Comprehension

## 11.1 What RC Tests

Q: Why does Reading Comprehension matter for law school?
A: Law school revolves around reading dense, unfamiliar text — judicial opinions, statutes, secondary sources — and extracting structure and argument quickly. RC simulates that workload under time pressure to predict who can handle it.

## 11.2 Section Structure

Q: How is the modern RC section structured?
A: Four passages, ~27 questions total, 35 minutes. One passage is a "comparative reading" pair (two shorter passages on a related topic). Roughly 8–9 minutes per passage including questions.

C: An RC section contains [4] passages and ~[27] questions in [35] minutes.

## 11.3 The Four Passage Subjects

Q: What four subject areas does RC draw from?
A: (1) Law / legal theory. (2) Humanities (literature, philosophy, art history). (3) Natural sciences (biology, physics, astronomy). (4) Social sciences (economics, sociology, history). Each test typically includes one of each.

## 11.4 Active Reading

Q: What does "active reading" mean for RC?
A: Reading with explicit goals — find the main point, track the author's attitude, note structural shifts — rather than reading passively for facts. Active readers finish passages with a clear mental map; passive readers must re-read for every question.

C: Active reading focuses on identifying the [main point], [author's attitude], and [structure], not memorizing details.

## 11.5 The Five Things to Track Per Passage

Q: What five elements should you track while reading any RC passage?
A: (1) Main Point — the central thesis. (2) Author's Attitude — supportive, neutral, critical. (3) Structure — how paragraphs relate. (4) Viewpoints — author vs others quoted. (5) Tone shifts — words like "however", "yet", "but" that mark turns.

## 11.6 Main Point

Q: How do you identify the main point of an RC passage?
A: Look for a sentence that captures the author's central claim — usually in the first or last paragraph. The main point is broad enough to cover the whole passage but specific enough to distinguish it from a topic sentence.

C: The main point is the author's [central thesis] across the entire passage.

## 11.7 Author's Attitude

Q: How do you track the author's attitude?
A: Note charged words ("admirable", "misguided", "compelling", "flawed"). Distinguish the author's view from quoted views. The author's attitude shapes which answer choices feel right vs out of character.

## 11.8 Passage Structure

Q: What are common RC structures?
A: (1) Defend a position by refuting alternatives. (2) Present two views and side with one. (3) Survey a phenomenon and analyze its causes. (4) Describe an old approach, criticize it, propose a new one. (5) Compare two analogous cases.

## 11.9 Question Types in RC

Q: What are the seven main RC question types?
A: (1) Main Point. (2) Author's Attitude / Tone. (3) Specific Detail. (4) Inference. (5) Function (purpose of a paragraph or phrase). (6) Analogy / Application. (7) Strengthen / Weaken.

## 11.10 Main Point Question

Q: How do you handle a Main Point question?
A: Avoid answers that are too narrow (cover only one paragraph), too broad (cover topic but not author's stance), or off-axis (capture detail but not the central thesis). The right answer reflects the entire passage at the right level of abstraction.

## 11.11 Specific Detail Question

Q: How do you handle a Specific Detail question?
A: Return to the relevant lines and read for context, not just the cited line. Wrong answers often recombine words from the passage in misleading ways. The correct answer paraphrases what the passage actually says.

C: For Specific Detail questions, always [return to the passage] and read the cited section in context.

## 11.12 Inference Question

Q: How does RC inference differ from LR inference?
A: Same standard — must follow from the passage — but the "stimulus" is much longer. Wrong answers often cite real passage material but draw conclusions the passage doesn't support. The right answer is supported by specific text you can point to.

## 11.13 Function Question

Q: What does a Function question ask?
A: Why did the author include this paragraph, sentence, or example? The answer describes the rhetorical purpose — to introduce a counterexample, to qualify a previous claim, to set up the next argument — not the content.

C: A Function answer describes [why] the author included a passage element, not [what] it says.

## 11.14 Analogy / Application Question

Q: What does an Analogy question ask?
A: It gives a hypothetical scenario from outside the passage and asks which scenario is most analogous to something in the passage. You must abstract the passage's structure and find the answer matching it.

## 11.15 Comparative Reading

Q: How do comparative-reading passages work?
A: Two shorter passages (A and B) on a related topic. Questions ask about each individually, both together, and points of agreement or disagreement. Note before answering: what each author claims, where they agree, where they differ.

## 11.16 Reading Speed vs Comprehension

Q: How do you balance reading speed against comprehension in RC?
A: Read for structure on the first pass — main point, attitude, paragraph roles. Don't try to memorize details. Return to the passage for any specific detail or inference question. This is faster than slow reading because it minimizes re-reading.

## 11.17 Pre-Phrasing in RC

Q: Can you pre-phrase RC answers?
A: Yes for Main Point and Author's Attitude — these you should state in your own words after reading. For Detail and Inference, pre-phrase based on the cited section after returning to the text.

## 11.18 Trap: Half-Right in RC

Q: How does the half-right trap appear in RC?
A: A long answer with one perfectly accurate clause and one inaccurate one. The accurate clause hooks you; the inaccurate clause kills the answer. Read every clause of every long answer.

## 11.19 Trap: Author Attribution

Q: What is the author-attribution trap?
A: The answer attributes a view to the author that actually belongs to a critic the author quoted (or vice versa). Track speaker carefully — "the author argues" is wrong if the passage said "some scholars contend".

## 11.20 RC Strategy

P: How do you efficiently work an RC passage?
S: Apply IPEE.

**IDENTIFY** — Note the four passages and their relative difficulty. Start with whichever subject feels most familiar to bank early points.

**PLAN** — Read the passage once at moderate speed, focused on structure and main point. Annotate sparingly — mark transitions, attitude words, and viewpoint shifts.

**EXECUTE** — Answer Main Point and Attitude questions from memory. For Detail, Inference, and Function questions, return to the relevant lines and read in context.

**EVALUATE** — Track time per passage. If you fall behind, pick the next-easiest passage and accept guessing on the hardest one — finishing three passages well beats half-finishing all four.
