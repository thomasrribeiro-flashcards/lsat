# LSAT — Causal Reasoning

## 5.1 Why Causal Arguments Dominate LR

Q: Why do causal arguments appear so often on the LSAT?
A: Real-world reasoning — including legal reasoning — depends heavily on attributing causes to events. The LSAT tests whether you can spot the gap between "X and Y are related" and "X caused Y", a gap legal advocates exploit constantly.

## 5.2 Correlation vs Causation

Q: What is the difference between correlation and causation?
A: Correlation means two phenomena vary together; causation means one produces the other. Correlation can arise without causation through coincidence, reverse causation, or a common third cause. The LSAT routinely presents correlations and concludes causation.

C: Two phenomena varying together establishes [correlation] but not [causation].

## 5.3 The Causal-Conclusion Indicator

Q: What language signals a causal conclusion?
A: Words like "caused", "produced", "led to", "responsible for", "because of", "due to", and "the reason for" indicate the author concludes one thing causes another. Spotting this language flags the argument as causal and triggers your causal toolkit.

C: An argument concluding that one thing "led to" another is a [causal] argument.

## 5.4 Five Ways a Causal Argument Can Fail

Q: What are the five standard ways a causal conclusion can fail?
A: (1) Coincidence — the correlation is chance. (2) Reverse causation — Y caused X, not X caused Y. (3) Common cause — a third factor Z caused both. (4) Selection bias — the sample doesn't represent the population. (5) Confounding variable — another factor accompanying X did the work.

## 5.5 Reverse Causation

Q: What is reverse causation and how do you spot it?
A: Reverse causation is when the argument has the causal arrow backwards — Y actually caused X. It's most plausible when X and Y could plausibly produce each other (e.g., wealth → happiness vs happiness → wealth).

C: When an argument concludes X causes Y but in reality Y caused X, the flaw is [reverse causation].

## 5.6 Common Cause (Third Variable)

Q: What is the common-cause objection?
A: A third variable Z causes both X and Y, producing the correlation between them without X causing Y. Classic example: ice-cream sales and drowning correlate, but summer heat causes both.

C: When a third factor produces both correlated phenomena, the flaw is the [common cause] (or third variable).

## 5.7 Coincidence (Chance)

Q: How does the coincidence objection weaken a causal argument?
A: Two events can co-occur without any underlying connection, especially in small samples or when many possible correlations exist. Pointing out that the apparent pattern is statistically unremarkable undermines the inference.

## 5.8 Strengthening a Causal Argument

Q: What kinds of evidence strengthen a causal argument?
A: (1) Eliminating alternative causes. (2) Showing the cause and effect occur together repeatedly. (3) Showing the effect disappears when the cause is removed. (4) Showing the cause precedes the effect. (5) Demonstrating a mechanism.

C: Strengthening a causal argument typically involves [eliminating alternative explanations] for the observed correlation.

## 5.9 Weakening a Causal Argument

Q: What kinds of evidence weaken a causal argument?
A: (1) Identifying a plausible alternative cause. (2) Showing reverse causation is possible. (3) Showing the effect occurs without the cause. (4) Showing the cause occurs without the effect. (5) Pointing to a flawed sample.

C: Weakening a causal argument typically involves presenting an [alternative cause] for the same effect.

## 5.10 Cause-Without-Effect / Effect-Without-Cause

Q: How does an unmatched case weaken a causal claim?
A: If X is supposedly the cause of Y but X has occurred without Y, X is not sufficient for Y. If Y has occurred without X, X is not necessary for Y. Either undermines a strong causal claim.

## 5.11 Sample-Based Causal Claims

Q: When a causal study cites a sample, what should you scrutinize?
A: Check sample size, representativeness, and selection method. A causal claim drawn from a tiny or self-selected sample (e.g., volunteers) may not generalize to the broader population the conclusion targets.

## 5.12 Pre-Phrasing Causal Answers

P: How should you approach a causal Strengthen or Weaken question?
S: Apply IPEE.

**IDENTIFY** — Read the stimulus and verify the conclusion is causal (look for "caused", "led to", etc.). Note the cause (X) and effect (Y).

**PLAN** — For Weaken, pre-phrase by asking: "What alternative cause could produce Y?" "Could Y have caused X?" "Could a common factor cause both?" For Strengthen, ask: "What rules out those alternatives?"

**EXECUTE** — Scan answer choices and match to your pre-phrased categories. Eliminate choices that target a non-causal aspect of the argument.

**EVALUATE** — Confirm the chosen answer addresses the causal link, not background facts. If two choices look correct, prefer the one that most directly attacks or supports the cause-effect connection.

## 5.13 Trap: "Cause Mentioned" Choice

Q: What is the "cause mentioned" trap on causal questions?
A: A wrong answer that simply restates the cause or effect from the stimulus without adding an alternative. The LSAT writes these because students confuse familiarity with correctness. Always check that the answer changes the causal landscape.

## 5.14 Trap: Out-of-Scope Strengthener

Q: What is the out-of-scope trap on strengthen causal questions?
A: An answer that supports a related but distinct causal claim — e.g., supporting that X causes Z when the conclusion is X causes Y. The answer must support the specific causal link in the conclusion.
