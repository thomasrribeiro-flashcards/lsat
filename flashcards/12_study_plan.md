# LSAT — Study Plan and Test Prep

## 12.1 Why Methodology Beats Hours

Q: Why do many test-takers plateau despite hundreds of hours of prep?
A: Without deliberate practice — analyzing each missed question, drilling specific weaknesses, and reviewing wrong-answer patterns — additional hours just reinforce existing habits. A focused 200 hours typically beats an unfocused 500.

## 12.2 The Three Phases of Prep

Q: What are the three phases of effective LSAT preparation?
A: (1) Foundations — learn question types, conditional logic, RC structures (4–8 weeks). (2) Drilling — practice question sets by type, untimed then timed (6–12 weeks). (3) Full-length practice tests with thorough review (4–8 weeks).

C: Effective LSAT prep moves from [foundations] to [drilling] to [full-length practice tests].

## 12.3 Diagnostic Test First

Q: Why take a diagnostic test before studying?
A: A baseline tells you which sections need most work, projects how much improvement is realistic, and gives you something to measure progress against. Without it, you can't tell whether your prep is working.

## 12.4 Blind Review (BR)

Q: What is Blind Review and why is it the highest-leverage practice habit?
A: After completing a timed section but before checking answers, redo every question you flagged as uncertain — this time without time pressure. Compare your timed answer to your blind-review answer to your final-answer key. The gap reveals whether errors come from time pressure (timing) or understanding (content).

C: Blind Review separates [timing errors] from [content errors] by redoing flagged questions untimed before checking the key.

## 12.5 The Wrong-Answer Journal

Q: What is a wrong-answer journal?
A: For every missed question, record: (1) question type, (2) why the right answer is right, (3) why the wrong answer you picked is wrong, (4) what trap pattern caught you, (5) one sentence on how to spot it next time. Reviewing this journal weekly accelerates learning more than doing new questions.

## 12.6 Drill by Question Type

Q: Why drill by question type rather than full sections?
A: Mixed-type sections train pacing but not pattern recognition. Drilling 30 strengthen questions in a row trains your eye to spot the structure of strengthen problems quickly — a skill that doesn't develop from one or two per section.

C: Drilling by [question type] develops pattern recognition faster than mixed-section practice.

## 12.7 Untimed Before Timed

Q: Why drill untimed before timed?
A: Speed comes from confidence in process. If you can't reliably answer a question type correctly with no time pressure, adding pressure won't fix it. Build accuracy first, then layer in pacing.

## 12.8 Timed Sections vs Full PTs

Q: When do you transition from drilling to full Practice Tests (PTs)?
A: When you can complete single sections at >85% accuracy untimed and >75% timed. PTs add the cognitive endurance dimension that single sections don't test — your accuracy on the fourth section often differs sharply from your first.

## 12.9 PT Frequency

Q: How often should you take full PTs?
A: 1–2 per week during the drilling phase, scaling up to 2–3 per week in the final 6 weeks. More frequent PTs prevent depth-of-review per test; less frequent PTs leave endurance untrained.

## 12.10 Score Plateaus

Q: What causes the typical score plateau, and how do you break through?
A: Plateaus form when prep recycles the same skills. Breakthroughs come from identifying a specific weakness (e.g., parallel reasoning, comparative passages), drilling it intensively for 1–2 weeks, then re-incorporating into mixed practice.

## 12.11 The 80-Page Rule

Q: Why do many test-takers improve more from re-doing old questions than from fresh ones?
A: Each official LSAT question is a precision-engineered diagnostic. Re-doing a missed question after two months tests whether the lesson stuck. Fresh questions test the same patterns, but old missed questions provide measurable learning evidence.

## 12.12 The 7Sage / LSATDemon / PowerScore Question

Q: How do you choose a prep platform?
A: Pick one comprehensive resource and stick with it. Platform-shopping wastes weeks. Common choices: 7Sage (analytics-heavy, video-based), LSAT Demon (drilling-focused, large library), PowerScore Bibles (book-based, deep theory). Any one of these can get you to 170+ if used fully.

## 12.13 Foundations of Logic Books

Q: What pre-LSAT logic resources are worth reading?
A: Hurley's Concise Introduction to Logic for formal logic basics. The PowerScore LR Bible for LSAT-specific frameworks. Skip pop-logic books — they don't map onto LSAT question structures.

## 12.14 Mental Game and Test-Day Anxiety

Q: How do you manage test-day anxiety?
A: Take 5+ full PTs at test-day conditions (same time of day, full timing, no music, simulated proctor screen). Familiarity is the strongest anxiety reducer. On test day, treat the unscored experimental section as scored — pretending it isn't may haunt the rest of the test.

## 12.15 The Last Two Weeks

Q: What should you do in the final two weeks before test day?
A: (1) 2–3 PTs per week, full review. (2) Light targeted drilling on remaining weaknesses. (3) Consistent sleep schedule matching test-day wake time. (4) Stop new content; focus on refining instincts. Cramming new material in week one usually hurts.

## 12.16 Test Day Logistics

Q: What test-day logistics matter for the online LSAT?
A: (1) Test environment cleared per LSAC rules. (2) Wired internet preferred over Wi-Fi. (3) Two screens are not allowed; close all extra monitors. (4) Bathroom break only between sections, not during. (5) ProctorU check-in takes ~30 min — log in early.

## 12.17 Score Cancellation

Q: When should you cancel a score?
A: Cancel only if you experienced a major disruption (technical, medical) or knowingly bombed multiple sections. Cancel within 6 days of test. Note: the cancellation appears on your record. Most test-takers underestimate their score on test day — wait for the report before deciding.

## 12.18 Retaking the LSAT

Q: How do law schools view multiple LSAT scores?
A: Most ABA-accredited schools consider only the highest score. LSAC reports all scores from the past 5 years on your CAS report. Plan to retake if your practice average is meaningfully higher than your test score, or if your score is below your target school's median.

C: Most law schools consider only your [highest] LSAT score, not the average.

## 12.19 LSAT-Flex / Online vs In-Person

Q: Which test format should you choose in 2026?
A: As of 2026, LSAT is administered exclusively through the online proctored format (LSAC LawHub). There is no in-person option for most administrations. Plan your test environment accordingly.

## 12.20 Setting a Realistic Score Goal

Q: How do you set a realistic LSAT score goal?
A: Identify your target schools' 75th-percentile LSAT (called the "75th"). Aim for that or higher. The gap between your diagnostic and 75th tells you whether 3 months or 9 months is more realistic. A 10-point improvement is common with diligent prep; 20+ points takes a year or more for most.

## 12.21 The Full Study Plan

P: How do you build a 6-month LSAT prep plan from scratch?
S: Apply IPEE.

**IDENTIFY** — Take a diagnostic. Set a target score from your school's 75th. Compute the gap and commit to weekly study hours (typical: 15–25/week for a 10-point gain).

**PLAN** — Months 1–2: foundations (LR theory, RC structure, conditional logic). Months 3–4: drilling by question type, untimed then timed. Months 5–6: 2 PTs/week with thorough Blind Review and a wrong-answer journal.

**EXECUTE** — Track every PT score and section accuracy. Re-do missed questions from PTs taken 2 months earlier each week. Stop when adding hours doesn't improve scores.

**EVALUATE** — Two weeks before test day, your last 5 PT average should be near your target. If not, postpone — a delayed test usually beats a low score on your record.

## 12.22 What Not to Do

Q: What are the most common LSAT-prep mistakes?
A: (1) Burning through PTs without thorough review. (2) Skipping Blind Review. (3) Switching prep platforms. (4) Cramming in week one. (5) Studying only strengths to feel productive. (6) Underestimating RC because LR is more popular online. (7) Taking the test before practice scores match the goal.
