# LSAT — Assumption Questions

## 6.1 What an Assumption Is

Q: What does "assumption" mean on the LSAT?
A: An assumption is a claim the author must rely on, but does not state, for the argument to work. Assumptions sit in the gap between what the premises say and what the conclusion claims, silently bridging the two.

C: An assumption is an [unstated premise] the argument depends on.

## 6.2 The Two Assumption Question Types

Q: What are the two assumption question types on the LSAT?
A: Necessary Assumption ("which of the following is required by the argument?") and Sufficient Assumption ("which of the following, if assumed, allows the conclusion to follow?"). They sound similar but require different answers.

C: The two assumption question types are [Necessary] Assumption and [Sufficient] Assumption.

## 6.3 Necessary Assumption Defined

Q: What makes an assumption "necessary"?
A: A necessary assumption is one without which the argument cannot work. If you remove or deny it, the argument falls apart. Necessary assumptions are typically modest claims — just enough to keep the logic intact.

C: A necessary assumption is one whose denial [destroys] the argument.

## 6.4 Sufficient Assumption Defined

Q: What makes an assumption "sufficient"?
A: A sufficient assumption is one which, if added as a premise, makes the conclusion follow logically. Sufficient assumptions are typically broad claims — more than the argument strictly needs, but enough to seal the gap.

C: A sufficient assumption, when added as a premise, makes the conclusion [logically follow].

## 6.5 Necessary vs Sufficient: The Key Difference

Q: Why must you distinguish necessary from sufficient assumption questions?
A: A correct sufficient-assumption answer is often too strong to be a necessary assumption — and a correct necessary answer is often too weak to make the conclusion follow. Picking the wrong type is the classic trap.

## 6.6 Necessary Assumption Stems

Q: What question stems signal a Necessary Assumption question?
A: "The argument requires assuming which of the following?" "Which is an assumption on which the argument depends?" "The argument's conclusion follows logically only if which is assumed?" — wait, that last one is sufficient. Necessary stems use words like "required", "depends on", "relies on".

C: Stems with "requires", "depends on", or "relies on" indicate a [Necessary] Assumption question.

## 6.7 Sufficient Assumption Stems

C: Stems with "if assumed", "if true", or "the conclusion follows logically if which is assumed" indicate a [Sufficient] Assumption question.

Q: Why does "if assumed" signal sufficient rather than necessary?
A: "If assumed" tests what happens when you add the choice as a premise. If adding it makes the conclusion airtight, it was sufficient. The question doesn't test whether the argument needs that exact claim — only whether adding it works.

## 6.8 The Negation Test

Q: What is the negation test and when do you use it?
A: For Necessary Assumption questions, negate each answer choice. If the negated version destroys the argument, that choice is the necessary assumption. If the negated version leaves the argument intact, the choice was not necessary.

C: The Negation Test confirms a necessary assumption: when negated, it must [destroy] the argument.

## 6.9 Negating Quantifiers Correctly

Q: How do you correctly negate quantifier-based answer choices for the negation test?
A: "All Xs are Ys" → "Some X is not Y". "Some Xs are Ys" → "No Xs are Ys". "Most Xs are Ys" → "At most half of Xs are Ys". Bad negations (turning "all" into "no" or "some" into "all") give wrong test results.

## 6.10 Why Negation Works

Q: Why does the negation test correctly identify necessary assumptions?
A: A necessary assumption must be true for the argument to function. If you suppose it false (negate it) and the argument still holds, it wasn't actually required. If denying it breaks the argument, it was load-bearing — i.e., necessary.

## 6.11 Pre-Phrasing the Gap

Q: Why is pre-phrasing the assumption gap before reading answers so valuable?
A: The LSAT writes attractive wrong answers designed to look reasonable in isolation. If you pre-phrase the gap between premises and conclusion, you can match against your prediction and dismiss tempting distractors quickly.

## 6.12 New-Term Bridges

Q: What is a "new-term" gap and why does it indicate the assumption?
A: When the conclusion contains a term that does not appear in any premise, the argument needs an assumption bridging the new term to something in the premises. Spotting the new term often gives you the assumption directly.

C: When the conclusion contains a term absent from the premises, the assumption must [bridge] that new term.

## 6.13 The Defender Assumption

Q: What is a "defender" assumption?
A: A defender assumption rules out a specific objection that would otherwise weaken the argument. Instead of supplying the missing logical link, it defends against an alternative explanation. Common in causal arguments.

C: A defender assumption [rules out] an alternative explanation that could otherwise undermine the argument.

## 6.14 The Supporter Assumption

Q: What is a "supporter" assumption?
A: A supporter assumption affirmatively supplies a logical link the argument needs. It functions like a missing premise that, combined with stated premises, gets you to the conclusion. Common in conditional and percentage arguments.

## 6.15 Trap: Too Strong on Necessary

Q: What is the "too strong" trap on Necessary Assumption questions?
A: The wrong answer makes a sweeping claim ("all", "always", "must always") that the argument doesn't need. Negating it would destroy a stronger argument, but the actual argument only needs a weaker version. Necessary assumptions are typically modest.

## 6.16 Trap: Too Weak on Sufficient

Q: What is the "too weak" trap on Sufficient Assumption questions?
A: The wrong answer is consistent with the argument but doesn't actually let the conclusion follow. The answer must be strong enough that adding it as a premise makes the conclusion inescapable.

## 6.17 IPEE for Assumption Questions

P: How do you tackle a Necessary Assumption question?
S: Apply IPEE.

**IDENTIFY** — Spot the question type from the stem ("requires" / "depends on"). Read the stimulus and locate the conclusion and premises.

**PLAN** — Find the gap: what unstated claim must hold for the premises to support the conclusion? Pre-phrase it before looking at answers.

**EXECUTE** — Match answers against your pre-phrase. For finalists, apply the negation test: negate the answer and ask whether the argument still works.

**EVALUATE** — Confirm the surviving answer is modest enough to be necessary, not stronger than required. Reject any choice introducing a new term not relevant to the gap.
