# LSAT — Test Overview

## 1.1 Purpose of the LSAT

Q: Why does the LSAT exist as a separate test from undergraduate GPA?
A: Law schools needed a standardized predictor of first-year law school performance because GPAs vary across institutions and majors. The LSAT measures reading comprehension and logical reasoning under time pressure — skills central to legal education — giving admissions a common yardstick.

C: The LSAT's primary stated purpose is to predict [first-year law school grades].

## 1.2 Scoring Scale

Q: Why is the LSAT scored on a 120–180 scale rather than a percentage?
A: The scaled score adjusts for slight difficulty differences across test forms so that a 165 on one administration represents the same ability as a 165 on another. The 120–180 band gives fine-grained discrimination at the high end where most law-school competition lives.

C: The LSAT scaled score range is [120] to [180].

Q: What is the approximate median LSAT score?
A: The median is around 152, the midpoint of the 120–180 scale. Top-14 law schools typically expect 170+, while many ABA-accredited schools admit candidates in the 150s.

## 1.3 Sections of the Modern LSAT

Q: Why was Logic Games (Analytical Reasoning) removed from the scored LSAT in August 2024?
A: A 2019 settlement with blind test-takers required LSAC to find a non-visual format. Rather than redesign games, LSAC replaced them with a second Logical Reasoning section, since LR skills better correlate with legal practice anyway.

C: As of August 2024 the scored LSAT consists of two [Logical Reasoning] sections and one [Reading Comprehension] section, plus one unscored experimental section.

Q: What is the unscored experimental section and why does it exist?
A: One section on the test is unscored and used by LSAC to pretest new questions for future exams. Test-takers cannot identify which section it is, so every section must be treated as scored.

## 1.4 Section Timing

C: Each scored LSAT section lasts [35] minutes.

Q: Why does the strict 35-minute timing matter strategically?
A: With ~25 questions per section, that gives roughly 80 seconds per question. Pacing decisions — when to skip, when to guess — often matter more than raw skill, because finishing all questions usually beats perfectly answering only two-thirds.

## 1.5 The Writing Sample

Q: Why is the LSAT Writing sample unscored but still required?
A: Schools receive the essay alongside the score and use it to compare your unedited argumentative writing against your polished personal statement. It's a quality-check on authorship, not a quantitative input.

C: LSAT Writing is [unscored] and completed [separately] from the multiple-choice exam, but is required for score release.

## 1.6 Raw Score vs Scaled Score

Q: How does a raw score become a scaled score?
A: Each correct answer adds one to the raw score (no penalty for wrong answers). LSAC then applies a difficulty-adjusted conversion table for that specific test form to map the raw count onto 120–180.

C: The LSAT has [no penalty for wrong answers], so guessing on every blank is strictly better than leaving them empty.

## 1.7 LSAT-Flex and Online Format

Q: Why did LSAT move to a remotely-proctored format?
A: Pandemic-era LSAT-Flex demonstrated that remote proctoring worked, and LSAC made the online format permanent. Test-takers now use their own computer with live human proctoring through video.

## 1.8 Test Day Strategy

P: How should you allocate effort across the three scored sections of an LSAT administration?
S: Apply IPEE.

**IDENTIFY** — Two LR sections and one RC section, all scored equally. One additional unscored experimental section is indistinguishable from a scored one.

**PLAN** — Treat every section as scored. Within each, plan to finish all 25 questions even if it means guessing on the hardest 2–3.

**EXECUTE** — Use a first-pass / second-pass approach: answer easy questions first, mark hard ones, return with remaining time. Fill every blank before time expires.

**EVALUATE** — Track timing per section in practice. If you regularly miss the last 3–5 questions, that's lost easy points; raise pacing before raising accuracy.
