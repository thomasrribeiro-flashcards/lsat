# LSAT — Conditional Logic

## 3.1 The Conditional Statement

Q: Why is conditional logic central to the LSAT?
A: Conditionals appear in nearly every Logical Reasoning section, both in stimuli and in answer choices. Misreading a conditional — especially confusing necessary with sufficient — is the single most common avoidable error on the test.

C: A conditional statement has the form "if [sufficient], then [necessary]".

## 3.2 Sufficient Condition

Q: What does "sufficient" mean in a conditional?
A: A sufficient condition is one whose truth guarantees the truth of the necessary condition. If the sufficient is true, the necessary must be true. Sufficient does not mean "the only way" — there can be many sufficient conditions for the same necessary one.

C: In "If A then B", A is the [sufficient] condition.

## 3.3 Necessary Condition

Q: What does "necessary" mean in a conditional?
A: A necessary condition is one that must hold for the sufficient to be true. Without the necessary, the sufficient cannot occur. Necessary does not mean "enough on its own" — it just means "required".

C: In "If A then B", B is the [necessary] condition.

## 3.4 Standard Notation

Q: How is "If A then B" typically diagrammed on the LSAT?
A: Test-takers write it as A → B, with the arrow pointing from sufficient to necessary. Diagramming forces you to commit to which term is which before working with the conditional.

C: "If A then B" is diagrammed as [A → B].

## 3.5 The Contrapositive

Q: What is the contrapositive of "If A then B"?
A: The contrapositive is "If not B then not A", written ¬B → ¬A. The contrapositive is logically equivalent to the original — if one is true, the other must be true. It is the only valid inference you can mechanically draw from a single conditional.

C: The contrapositive of A → B is [¬B → ¬A].

## 3.6 Mistaken Reversal

Q: What is the mistaken reversal of "If A then B"?
A: The mistaken reversal is "If B then A" (B → A). This is invalid — knowing the necessary condition holds tells you nothing about whether the sufficient holds, because other sufficient conditions could have produced B.

C: Concluding B → A from A → B is called a [mistaken reversal].

## 3.7 Mistaken Negation

Q: What is the mistaken negation of "If A then B"?
A: The mistaken negation is "If not A then not B" (¬A → ¬B). This is invalid for the same reason as mistaken reversal — the absence of one sufficient condition doesn't rule out other sufficient conditions for B.

C: Concluding ¬A → ¬B from A → B is called a [mistaken negation].

## 3.8 Sufficient-Indicator Words

Q: What words introduce the sufficient condition?
A: "If", "when", "whenever", "every", "all", "any", and "in order to" introduce the sufficient. Whatever follows these words goes on the left of the arrow.

C: The words "if", "when", and "every" introduce the [sufficient] condition.

## 3.9 Necessary-Indicator Words

C: The words "only", "only if", "requires", "must", "depends on", and "unless" introduce the [necessary] condition.

Q: Why is "only if" tricky?
A: "Only if" introduces the necessary condition, even though "if" alone introduces the sufficient. "A only if B" means A → B, not B → A. The "only" hijacks the if and reverses what students naturally assume.

## 3.10 The "Unless" Construction

Q: How do you diagram "A unless B"?
A: Translate "unless" as "if not". So "A unless B" becomes "If not B then A" (¬B → A), or equivalently the contrapositive ¬A → B. This rule applies uniformly regardless of which clause sits where in the sentence.

C: "A unless B" is diagrammed as [¬B → A].

## 3.11 Conditional Chains

Q: What inference can you draw from "A → B" combined with "B → C"?
A: You can chain them into A → C. Conditional chains are fully transitive: if A guarantees B and B guarantees C, then A guarantees C. The contrapositive ¬C → ¬A also holds.

C: From A → B and B → C, you can validly conclude [A → C].

## 3.12 "Each", "All", "Every" as Universal Conditionals

Q: How do "all", "every", and "each" function as conditionals?
A: "All Xs are Ys" translates to X → Y. "Every student passed" means "If student then passed". Treating universal claims as conditionals lets you take contrapositives and chain them like any other conditional.

## 3.13 "No" as a Negated Conditional

Q: How do you diagram "No As are Bs"?
A: "No A is B" means A → ¬B (and equivalently B → ¬A). Two categories declared mutually exclusive give you a conditional from either side to the negation of the other.

C: "No As are Bs" is diagrammed as [A → ¬B].

## 3.14 Biconditionals

Q: What is a biconditional and what indicators signal one?
A: A biconditional is "A if and only if B" — A → B and B → A both hold. Indicator phrases include "if and only if", "exactly when", and "all and only". Biconditionals are rare on the LSAT but unmissable when present.

## 3.15 Negating a Conditional

Q: How do you negate the conditional "If A then B"?
A: The negation is "A and not B" — a single counterexample where the sufficient holds but the necessary fails. To deny a universal claim, you only need one case where it breaks.

C: The negation of A → B is the joint occurrence of [A and ¬B].
