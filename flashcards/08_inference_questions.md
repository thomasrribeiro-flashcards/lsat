# LSAT — Inference Questions

## 8.1 What an Inference Question Asks

Q: How does an inference question differ from an assumption or strengthen question?
A: Inference questions give you a stimulus of facts (not an argument) and ask what must be true based on those facts. There is no conclusion to attack or support — your job is to derive a conclusion the stimulus already establishes.

C: Inference questions ask what [must be true] (or most strongly supported) given the stimulus.

## 8.2 The Two Major Variants

Q: What are the two main inference question types?
A: "Must Be True" — looking for a statement that is logically guaranteed by the stimulus. "Most Strongly Supported" — looking for the statement most likely true given the facts, even if not strictly guaranteed.

C: The two main inference variants are [Must Be True] and [Most Strongly Supported].

## 8.3 Must-Be-True Stems

Q: What stems indicate a Must-Be-True question?
A: "Which of the following must be true?" "Which can be properly inferred?" "Which logically follows from the statements above?" These demand strict logical entailment from the stimulus.

## 8.4 Most-Strongly-Supported Stems

Q: What stems indicate a Most-Strongly-Supported question?
A: "Which is most strongly supported by the statements above?" "Which is the most reasonable conclusion?" These accept conclusions that are highly probable given the facts, even if not airtight.

C: Stems with "most strongly supported" or "most reasonably concluded" indicate MSS (Most Strongly Supported), a slightly [weaker] standard than Must Be True.

## 8.5 The Fact-Test

Q: What is the Fact-Test for inference answers?
A: The correct answer must be supported by facts in the stimulus. If you can't trace each part of the answer back to a stated fact, it fails. Outside knowledge is irrelevant.

C: The Fact-Test rule: every part of the inference answer must trace back to [the stimulus].

## 8.6 No New Information

Q: Why must inference answers stay within the stimulus?
A: The stimulus contains all the facts you may use. Any answer requiring additional assumptions or real-world knowledge is wrong, no matter how plausible, because the stem asks what follows from what was stated.

## 8.7 Cannot-Be-True Variant

Q: What does a "Cannot Be True" question ask?
A: It asks which answer is incompatible with the stimulus — that is, which one must be false. The four wrong answers are each consistent with the stimulus; only the correct answer is impossible given what was stated.

C: A Cannot-Be-True correct answer must be [false] given the stimulus.

## 8.8 The Trap of Wanting an Argument

Q: What is the most common error on inference questions?
A: Treating the stimulus as if it were an argument. Students try to weaken or strengthen the supposed conclusion. Inference stimuli are facts — your only job is to extract what those facts imply, not evaluate them.

## 8.9 Conditional Inference Stimuli

Q: How do you handle inference stimuli loaded with conditionals?
A: Diagram each conditional and chain them. The correct answer typically restates a chain you can build, or its contrapositive. Verify the answer matches a valid chain — not a mistaken reversal or negation.

## 8.10 Quantifier Inference Stimuli

Q: How do you handle inference stimuli with "all", "most", and "some"?
A: Apply the chaining rules from chapter 4. All + all → some. Most + most → some. Most + all (downstream) → most. Discard answers that require chains not on this list.

## 8.11 Trap: Could-Be-True Answers

Q: What is the "could be true" trap?
A: An answer choice consistent with the stimulus but not required by it. On Must-Be-True questions, this is wrong — you need entailment, not consistency. On MSS, you need probability, not just possibility.

## 8.12 Trap: Too-Strong Inference

Q: What is the "too strong" trap on inference?
A: An answer that overstates what the stimulus supports — adds "always", "must", "the only", or "never" beyond what the facts justify. The LSAT prefers carefully hedged inferences over sweeping ones.

C: The "too strong" trap uses absolutes like ["always" or "never"] that go beyond what the stimulus supports.

## 8.13 Trap: Out-of-Scope Inference

Q: What is the out-of-scope trap on inference?
A: An answer about a topic the stimulus didn't address. Even if true in the real world, it's wrong because the stimulus doesn't supply the facts. Outside knowledge cannot fill the gap.

## 8.14 Pre-Phrasing Inferences

Q: Should you pre-phrase inference questions?
A: Pre-phrasing strict inferences is hard because many things can follow from the same stimulus. Instead, internalize the relationships in the stimulus (chains, quantifier overlaps), then match any answer that fits those relationships exactly.

## 8.15 IPEE for Inference

P: How do you tackle a Must-Be-True question with multiple conditional statements?
S: Apply IPEE.

**IDENTIFY** — Read carefully and confirm there is no argument — only facts. Note conditionals, quantifiers, and direct factual claims.

**PLAN** — Diagram conditionals, take contrapositives, and identify any chains. Note any "all/most/some" relationships and what overlaps follow.

**EXECUTE** — Test each answer against your diagram. Eliminate any that requires information beyond the stimulus. Eliminate any that introduces an absolute the facts don't support.

**EVALUATE** — Confirm the surviving answer matches a chain or direct restatement of stimulus facts. If still uncertain, prefer the more modest claim — inferences are usually understated, not overstated.
