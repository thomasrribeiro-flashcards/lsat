# LSAT — Argument Structure

## 2.1 What an Argument Is

Q: In LSAT terminology, what makes a passage an "argument" rather than just a set of facts?
A: An argument presents at least one claim (the conclusion) supported by at least one other claim (a premise). Without a premise–conclusion relationship, you have a description, report, or set of opinions — not an argument.

C: Every LSAT argument contains at least one [premise] and exactly one main [conclusion].

## 2.2 Premise

Q: What is a premise?
A: A premise is a statement offered as evidence or support for the conclusion. The author treats premises as accepted facts; you don't need to verify whether they are actually true to evaluate the argument's logic.

C: A premise is a statement offered as [support] for the conclusion.

## 2.3 Conclusion

Q: What is the main conclusion of an LSAT argument?
A: The main conclusion is the single claim the entire argument is trying to establish. Every other claim either supports it directly or supports something that supports it. If you remove the conclusion, the argument has no point.

C: The main conclusion is the claim the argument is ultimately trying to [prove].

## 2.4 Premise Indicator Words

Q: What are common premise indicator words on the LSAT?
A: Words like "because", "since", "for", "given that", "as", "owing to", and "in light of" introduce premises. They signal that what follows is being offered as evidence rather than as the point being argued.

C: The words "because", "since", and "for" typically introduce a [premise].

## 2.5 Conclusion Indicator Words

C: The words "therefore", "thus", "hence", "so", and "consequently" typically introduce a [conclusion].

Q: Why are indicator words helpful but not decisive?
A: Indicators reliably point to one role most of the time, but authors sometimes omit them entirely or use "thus" mid-sentence in a non-conclusion way. When indicators are missing, ask "what is this argument trying to prove?" to find the conclusion.

## 2.6 The "Why Test" for Conclusion

P: How do you identify the main conclusion when no indicator words are present?
S: Apply IPEE.

**IDENTIFY** — A passage with multiple claims and no clear "therefore" or "because".

**PLAN** — Pick the candidate claim and ask: "Why should I believe this?" The other statements should answer that question.

**EXECUTE** — If the other claims provide reasons for the candidate, the candidate is the conclusion. If the candidate provides reason for one of the others, you have it backwards — swap them.

**EVALUATE** — Confirm by checking that no remaining claim supports anything except the chosen conclusion (directly or via an intermediate claim).

## 2.7 Sub-Conclusion (Intermediate Conclusion)

Q: What is a sub-conclusion?
A: A sub-conclusion is a statement that is supported by some premises and itself supports the main conclusion. It plays both roles — conclusion of one mini-argument and premise of the larger one.

C: A statement supported by premises that itself supports the main conclusion is called a [sub-conclusion] or [intermediate conclusion].

## 2.8 Counterpremise (Concession)

Q: What is a counterpremise and why do authors include them?
A: A counterpremise is a fact the author concedes that runs against the conclusion. Authors include them to acknowledge opposing evidence and then explain why the conclusion still holds despite that evidence — making the argument appear more balanced.

C: A statement that runs counter to the conclusion but is acknowledged by the author is called a [counterpremise].

## 2.9 Background Information

Q: What role does background information play in an argument?
A: Background information sets context but neither supports nor opposes the conclusion. Mistaking background for a premise leads you to misdiagnose what the argument actually depends on.

## 2.10 Opposing Viewpoints

Q: How do you handle a passage that presents two views, one belonging to "some people" and another to the author?
A: Identify which view is the author's — that view's conclusion is the main conclusion. Statements attributed to "some say" or "critics argue" are usually positions the author rejects, not premises supporting the conclusion.

C: When a passage starts "Some people claim X. However, …", the author's conclusion is signaled by the word [However].

## 2.11 Argument vs Explanation

Q: What is the difference between an argument and an explanation?
A: An argument tries to convince you that a claim is true. An explanation assumes the claim is already accepted and tells you why it is true. Mistaking one for the other leads to weakening or strengthening the wrong thing.

## 2.12 The Stimulus

Q: What is the LSAT "stimulus"?
A: The stimulus is the passage above the question stem — the argument or set of facts you must analyze. Reading the stimulus once carefully usually beats reading it twice quickly, because LR errors mostly come from misreading rather than reasoning.
