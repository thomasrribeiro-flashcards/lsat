# LSAT — Method, Role, Principle, and Parallel

## 9.1 Method-of-Reasoning Questions

Q: What does a Method-of-Reasoning question ask?
A: It asks how the argument proceeds — what technique it uses to reach its conclusion. The correct answer is an abstract description of the argument's structure, not its content.

C: A Method-of-Reasoning answer describes the argument's [structure] in abstract terms.

## 9.2 Method Stems

Q: What stems indicate a Method-of-Reasoning question?
A: "The argument proceeds by …" "The author's reasoning depends on which technique?" "Which best describes the method of argumentation?"

## 9.3 Common Methods

Q: What methods of reasoning does the LSAT test that argue from evidence toward a conclusion?
A: Drawing a generalization from cases. Citing an analogy. Appealing to authority. Inferring from a general principle to a specific case.

Q: What methods of reasoning does the LSAT test that argue against a position?
A: Refuting a position by counterexample. Eliminating alternatives.

## 9.4 Role-in-Argument Questions

Q: What does a Role-in-Argument question ask?
A: It points to a specific statement in the stimulus and asks what role that statement plays — premise, sub-conclusion, main conclusion, counterpremise, or background.

C: A Role-in-Argument answer identifies whether a specific statement is a premise, sub-conclusion, main conclusion, counterpremise, or [background].

## 9.5 Role Stems

Q: What stems indicate a Role-in-Argument question?
A: "The claim that … plays which of the following roles in the argument?" "The statement that … functions in the argument as …?" Often the cited statement appears in quotes or italics.

## 9.6 Diagnosing Roles

P: How do you diagnose the role of a cited statement?
S: Apply IPEE.

**IDENTIFY** — Locate the cited statement and identify the main conclusion of the argument separately.

**PLAN** — Ask: is the cited statement supported by other claims (sub-conclusion or main conclusion)? Does it support other claims (premise)? Does it run against the conclusion (counterpremise)? Or is it merely setup (background)?

**EXECUTE** — Match your diagnosis to the answer choices, watching for "supports the conclusion" (premise) vs "is the conclusion" vs "is a position the author opposes".

**EVALUATE** — Verify your diagnosis by trying to remove the statement: does the argument lose support? Lose its conclusion? Lose nothing? The effect tells you the role.

## 9.7 Principle Questions

Q: What is a "principle" in LSAT terms?
A: A principle is a general rule that applies across many situations. Principle questions ask either how a principle supports a specific conclusion (Principle-Strengthen) or how a specific case illustrates a principle (Principle-Inference).

C: A principle is a [general rule] that, when applied to facts, supports or generates a conclusion.

## 9.8 Identify-the-Principle Stems

Q: What stems indicate an Identify-the-Principle question?
A: "Which principle, if valid, most helps to justify the reasoning?" "The reasoning conforms to which of the following principles?" The answer is a general rule.

## 9.9 Apply-the-Principle Stems

Q: What stems indicate an Apply-the-Principle question?
A: "Which judgment most closely conforms to the principle stated above?" "Which action would most clearly violate the principle?" The stimulus gives the principle; the answer applies it.

## 9.10 Principle-Conformity

Q: What does it mean for an answer to "conform to" a principle?
A: The answer's facts must satisfy the principle's sufficient condition (triggering its necessary consequence), or its facts must be consistent with the principle's restrictions. Answers that violate, or are silent on, the principle's conditions don't conform.

## 9.11 Parallel-Reasoning Questions

Q: What does a Parallel-Reasoning question ask?
A: It asks which answer's argument has the same logical structure as the stimulus's argument. The correct answer matches in form even when subject matter differs entirely.

C: A Parallel-Reasoning answer matches the stimulus in [logical structure], not subject matter.

## 9.12 What Must Match in Parallel Reasoning

Q: What features must match between stimulus and parallel answer?
A: (1) Type of conclusion (conditional, causal, prediction, recommendation). (2) Strength of conclusion (must, probably, could). (3) Validity (valid stays valid, invalid stays invalid). (4) Logical structure of the premises.

## 9.13 What Doesn't Need to Match

Q: What features don't need to match in parallel reasoning?
A: Subject matter, order of premises, surface vocabulary, and number of words. Matching these instead of structure leads to wrong answers because the LSAT writes traps that rephrase the topic without preserving the logic.

## 9.14 Parallel-Flaw Questions

Q: How do Parallel-Flaw questions differ from Parallel-Reasoning?
A: The stimulus contains a flawed argument. You must find the answer that contains the same flaw — not just any flaw, but the same logical mistake. Identifying the specific flaw before scanning answers saves time.

C: In a Parallel-Flaw question, the correct answer must commit [the same flaw] as the stimulus.

## 9.15 Parallel Strategy

Q: What's the most efficient strategy for parallel-reasoning questions?
A: First, abstract the stimulus to letters or shapes. Then test each answer by abstracting it the same way. Two abstractions match if and only if the structures are identical. Skip subject-matter overlap as a tiebreaker — it's usually a trap.

## 9.16 Trap: Same Topic, Different Structure

Q: What is the "same topic" trap on parallel questions?
A: An answer that mentions the same subject as the stimulus but uses a different argument structure. The LSAT exploits the temptation to pick familiar wording. Always abstract first and match structure.

## 9.17 Point-at-Issue and Point-of-Agreement Questions

Q: What does a Point-at-Issue question ask?
A: It gives two speakers' positions and asks what they specifically disagree about. The correct answer must be a claim each speaker has taken a position on, with their positions being opposite.

C: A Point-at-Issue answer must be a claim where the two speakers [take opposite positions], with each having addressed it.

Q: What does a Point-of-Agreement question ask?
A: It asks what both speakers would agree on. The correct answer must be a claim where both speakers have committed to the same position, even if they disagree on other things.
